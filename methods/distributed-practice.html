Distributed Practice

‚òÖ ‚òÖ ‚òÖ

Distributed Practice, also known as spaced practice, involves spreading study sessions over multiple days or weeks rather than cramming everything into one long session. This spacing effect dramatically improves long-term retention compared to massed practice.

üí° What It Is

Distributed practice is the opposite of cramming. Instead of studying for 6 hours the night before an exam, you study for 1 hour each day for 6 days. The total time is the same, but the spacing makes a huge difference in retention.

This technique leverages the "spacing effect"‚Äîthe finding that information is better remembered when study sessions are spaced out over time. The forgetting that occurs between sessions actually strengthens memory when you review again.

üß† Why It Works

When you space out your study sessions, you're repeatedly retrieving information from memory. Each time you recall something after a delay, you strengthen the memory trace. The effort required to retrieve information after forgetting makes the memory stronger.

Cramming creates the illusion of knowing because everything is fresh in your mind. But without repeated retrieval over time, the information quickly fades. Distributed practice creates durable, long-term memories.

Research consistently shows that distributed practice leads to better long-term retention than massed practice, even when total study time is equal (Cepeda et al., 2006).

üë§ Best For

  • Long-term learning and retention
  • Preparing for exams weeks or months in advance
  • Building durable knowledge bases
  • Learning material you'll need to remember long-term
  • Any subject where retention matters more than immediate performance

‚öôÔ∏è How To Use It

1

Plan Your Schedule

Start early. If you have 4 weeks until an exam, plan to study the material multiple times over those weeks, not just at the end.

2

Space Out Sessions

Study the same material on multiple days with gaps in between. A good rule: review after 1 day, then 3 days, then 1 week, then 2 weeks.

3

Use Active Recall

Don't just re-read. Test yourself on the material. The effort to recall after spacing is what strengthens memory.

4

Increase Intervals Gradually

Start with shorter gaps (1-2 days), then gradually increase to longer gaps (weeks) as you master the material.

5

Stick to the Schedule

Consistency is key. Don't skip sessions. Even 15-20 minutes of review is better than nothing.

  • Giving up because it feels slower than cramming
