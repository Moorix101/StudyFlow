Dual Coding

‚òÖ ‚òÖ ‚òÖ
Launch Tool

Dual Coding is a learning theory that suggests combining verbal and visual information creates stronger memory traces. By encoding information through both channels‚Äîwords and images‚Äîyou create multiple pathways to retrieve the information later.

üí° What It Is

Developed by psychologist Allan Paivio in the 1970s, Dual Coding Theory proposes that our brains process information through two separate but interconnected systems: one for verbal information (words, language) and one for non-verbal information (images, spatial information).

When you learn something using both systems simultaneously‚Äîfor example, reading about a concept while also looking at a diagram‚Äîyou create two memory codes instead of one. This redundancy makes the information easier to recall because you have multiple retrieval paths.

üß† Why It Works

Having two codes for the same information means if one pathway fails during retrieval, the other can still work. This redundancy significantly improves memory and understanding.

Visual and verbal information are processed in different brain regions. When both are activated together, they create stronger neural connections. The visual system can also help make abstract concepts more concrete and easier to understand.

Research consistently shows that combining text with images improves learning outcomes compared to text alone (Mayer, 2009).

üë§ Best For

  • Visual learners who benefit from diagrams and images
  • Complex or abstract concepts that need visualization
  • Subjects with spatial relationships (anatomy, geography, architecture)
  • Learning processes, systems, or workflows

‚öôÔ∏è How To Use It

1

Read and Understand

Start by reading the text or verbal explanation of the concept. Make sure you understand the basic information.

2

Create or Find Visuals

Draw diagrams, flowcharts, mind maps, or sketches that represent the same information. If visuals already exist, study them carefully.

3

Connect the Two

Actively link the verbal and visual information. Point to parts of the diagram while explaining them in words. Make explicit connections.

4

Practice Both Ways

Test yourself by describing the visual in words, then recreating the visual from the verbal description. This strengthens both codes.

5

Review Together

When reviewing, always look at both the text and visuals together. Don't separate them‚Äîthe power comes from the combination.
