Interleaving

‚òÖ ‚òÖ ‚òÖ
Launch Tool

Interleaving is a study technique where you mix different topics or types of problems during practice sessions instead of focusing on one type at a time. This counterintuitive approach improves your ability to distinguish between concepts and apply the right strategy.

üí° What It Is

Traditional studying often involves "blocking"‚Äîpracticing one type of problem repeatedly before moving to the next. Interleaving breaks this pattern by mixing different topics, problem types, or subjects within the same study session.

For example, instead of doing 20 algebra problems, then 20 geometry problems, you'd mix them: algebra, geometry, algebra, geometry, and so on. This forces your brain to constantly switch gears and identify which strategy to use.

üß† Why It Works

Interleaving improves discrimination‚Äîyour ability to tell similar concepts apart. When you practice problems in blocks, you can get into a "groove" and solve them almost automatically without really thinking about which strategy to use.

By mixing problems, you're forced to actively choose the right approach each time. This decision-making process strengthens your understanding of when and why to use each method, leading to better transfer to new situations.

Research shows that while interleaving feels harder and slower during practice, it leads to significantly better long-term retention and transfer (Rohrer, 2012).

üë§ Best For

  • Math and science problem-solving
  • Learning multiple similar concepts
  • Preparing for exams with mixed question types
  • Subjects where you need to choose between strategies

‚öôÔ∏è How To Use It

1

Identify Related Topics

Choose 2-4 related topics or problem types that you're learning. They should be similar enough to be confusing if mixed, but distinct enough to require different approaches.

2

Create a Mixed Practice Set

Instead of doing all problems of one type, create a list that alternates or randomly mixes the different types. Use a random number generator or shuffle your practice problems.

3

Practice with Switching

Work through the mixed set. Before each problem, pause and think: "What type is this? What strategy should I use?" This active decision-making is key.

4

Review Mistakes Carefully

When you make errors, analyze whether you chose the wrong strategy or applied it incorrectly. This feedback helps improve discrimination.

5

Space Out Sessions

Combine interleaving with spaced repetition. Return to mixed practice after a day or two to reinforce the discrimination skills.

üí™ Tips for Success

üé≤

Use Randomization

Shuffle your practice problems or use a random order. Don't create predictable patterns.

ü§î

Before each problem, identify the type and strategy. This active decision-making is crucial.

üìä

Note whether mistakes come from wrong strategy choice or execution. This helps improve discrimination.

Interleaving feels harder, but trust the process. The benefits show up in long-term retention.

üîÑ

Don't mix completely unrelated subjects. Focus on topics that could be confused with each other.

‚úÖ

Use interleaving with spaced repetition for maximum effectiveness.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

  • Mixing completely unrelated topics that don't benefit from discrimination practice
  • Creating predictable patterns instead of true randomization
  • Giving up because it feels harder than blocked practice
  • Not actively thinking about which strategy to use before solving
  • Using interleaving for brand new material (learn basics first, then interleave)
